# Design — Per-topic guides ("how to beat this topic")

Short, friendly help for each topic, reachable from the topic-selection screen.
Aimed at a confident **British 10-year-old** (Year 5/6). **Not verbose.**

## Tone & format

- **British English** (maths not math; £/p for money; "remember", "take away").
- Plain words, **short sentences**, encouraging, never patronising. No jargon.
- **Punchy, not wordy.** A whole guide should read in ~15 seconds.
- **Accuracy is paramount** — every tip and worked example must be correct.
  Babysitter audits each guide; wrong/woolly advice is a DoD failure.

### Structure (each guide = data, ~5 short lines max)

- `intro`: one line — what it is / why it's worth being quick at it.
- `tips`: **2–4** bullet strategies — the actual "be fast" tricks.
- `example`: one tiny worked example.
- (optional) one short encouraging line.

## Seed examples (match this style)

**Halves**
- intro: "Halving means splitting a number into two equal parts."
- tips:
  - "Split into tens and ones, halve each, add them. Half of 48 = 20 + 4 = 24."
  - "Odd numbers end in ·5. Half of 7 is 3·5."
  - "Halving twice is the same as finding a quarter."
  - "Know the everyday ones: half of 100 is 50, half of 60 is 30."
- example: "Half of 90 → 45 (90 minutes is one and a half hours)."

**Times**
- intro: "Knowing your tables off by heart speeds up almost every question."
- tips:
  - "Drill the tricky ones: 6×7=42, 7×8=56, 8×9=72."
  - "×9: do ×10 then take one lot off. 9×7 = 70 − 7 = 63."
  - "×4: double, then double again."
  - "Order doesn't matter: 3×8 is the same as 8×3."
- example: "7×6 → think 7×3=21, then double → 42."

## Strategy hints per current topic (for the builder to draft from)

- **Doubles** — double tens and ones separately (double 36 = 60 + 12 = 72);
  doubling 5s/25s; doubling is halving's twin.
- **Add & Subtract** — bridge through the next ten (47+38: 47+3=50, then +35);
  near-tens by rounding then adjusting (+9 = +10−1); count up for subtraction.
- **Add & Subtract II** — same tricks with hundreds; keep the hundreds, work the
  rest.
- **Number bonds** — the two parts make the whole; for 100 the ones add to 10 and
  the tens add to 9 (e.g. 63 → 37). Bonds to 10 are the foundation.
- **Number bonds II** — to 1000 use bonds to 100 then the hundreds; decimal bonds
  to 1 are just bonds to 10 in disguise (0·3 → 0·7).
- **Fractions (→ decimal)** — learn the key ones by heart: ½=0·5, ¼=0·25,
  ¾=0·75, ⅕=0·2, 1/10=0·1.
- **Squares** — a square is a number times itself; learn 1²–12²; 15²=225, 25²=625
  are handy.
